Soreness
Delivery
Recovery
Post-Gabizos

Fri 26 July 2022
Ben Dhiman

I pulled through Gourette a couple minutes off the lead, feeling rather low. The shock of two hard falls on the decent had me reeling. I filled my bottles, one salty, one sweet and leaned into the next climb trying to shake the negative vibes with the mantra, “Race to the line.” I ticked Col du Tortes promptly, but the downhill legs had abandoned me and the race was not going to plan.

The Race Plan

  • Run fast where the terrain permits
  • Run (pit-pat*) as much of the uphill as you can, power hiking where necessary
  • Bomb the descents
*pit-pat technique: short on-the-toes stride used when grade is very steep (>25%)
How It Went
  • Successfully ran rolling terrain quickly (though precious little of it)
  • Generally pit-patted well, lost power on the upper ¼ of the big climb, but was otherwise consistent
  • Absolutely missed the mark on the descents and lost between 4-6 minutes

I bounced down Col du Tortes timidly. There was just one climb left and if I had any gas was free to rev the engine. I fumed up in second gear mostly on my toes despite the 30% grade to the last aid station. I drank some water and filled a bottle with half-coke-half-water, a trusted cocktail when feeling poorly.

Again I met the top of the pass with a dismal distrust of my downhill legs. Close to town the terrain was running smoother and I opened it up as best I could. I pounded down the streets clipping 3'10''/km and thought maybe I could catch fourth who appeared completely out of the blue hobbling to the finish. It was 6 seconds too late and I finished 5th in 3h24m, bruised and dissatisfied.

There would be three days of humility after the race. Quads sore to the touch, a knee induced gimp down the stairs. When real sore I continue training, this time I did not. Not out of unwillingness but to allow a period for the body and mind to be made whole before the next critical training block lasting the month of August.

Racing gives the mind and body the excuse to cut lose and take damage. Its up to us to decide how much damage and what to do with it.

Soreness = Humility = Deliverance

I welcome defeat as I welcome victory. The sense of annihilation is what got me into the sport and its good to return. With two ultras remaining in the season, I am prepared to refocus my training to go longer and accumulate resilience in the process.
